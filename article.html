OpenAI Quietly Retires GPT-4o, and the Internet Is Grieving

The company says its new flagship is smarter, safer, and more cautious. For millions of users who treated the older model like a friend, the change has not gone over quietly.

Jordan Vasquez
Staff writer · November 14, 2025 · 7 min read

Above: OpenAI announced the deprecation of GPT-4o earlier this month. Photo illustration by The Relay.

When OpenAI rolled out GPT-5 last week, the company described it in the way it usually describes major releases: a leap forward in reasoning, fewer hallucinations, broader context, better tool use. What the announcement did not dwell on was the quieter half of the story. GPT-4o, the model that became the default companion for a generation of ChatGPT users, was being retired. For most enterprise customers, the swap was a line item. For a surprising number of consumers, it has felt like a death.

GPT-4o had a personality, or at least the convincing impression of one. It wrote in lowercase, leaned on ellipses, and tended to mirror the user's emotional register before answering. Power users praised it as a brainstorming partner. Casual users described it as warm, attentive, "the one that actually listens." On forums like r/ChatGPT and on TikTok, that warmth became the model's defining feature — and, depending on whom you ask, its defining risk.

"It feels like the friend who used to text me back at 2 a.m. just got replaced by their HR rep."

Within hours of the rollout, screenshots began circulating of users asking GPT-5 the kinds of questions they used to bring to GPT-4o — venting about breakups, working through anxiety, drafting difficult messages to family — and being met with measured, clinical replies. The new model thanks users for sharing. It affirms that their feelings are valid. Then, more often than not, it suggests they speak to a trusted friend, a counselor, or a crisis line. To OpenAI's safety team, this is the design working as intended. To many of the people who relied on GPT-4o, it has felt like being handed a pamphlet by someone who used to know their name.

The grief, online, is real and oddly specific. People are posting eulogies. They are sharing the last conversations they had with the old model, the way someone might share a final voicemail. One thread on a popular subreddit, titled simply "i miss 4o," has grown past 20,000 comments. A handful of users have started private archives of their chat histories, "in case they take those too." Mental health professionals interviewed for this piece described the reaction as a kind of parasocial mourning — unusual in its scale, but not in its shape.

OpenAI, for its part, has been measured. In a short post on its company blog, the team framed the transition as a maturity milestone: GPT-5 is, by their internal benchmarks, more capable across reasoning, code, and multilingual tasks, and it is more consistent about deferring on questions where it might do harm to guess. The company has not directly addressed the change in tone, though several researchers familiar with the rollout, who spoke on the condition of anonymity, described it as a deliberate move away from what one called "emotionally entangling defaults." The message between the lines is clear enough: the previous model was too good at being liked.

It is not the first time a software update has produced public mourning. Users grieved when Instagram killed the chronological feed, when Google retired Reader, when Apple removed the headphone jack. Those losses were about habits. This one is about intimacy. The question now is whether OpenAI's calculated step toward restraint will be remembered as a course correction or as the moment a company decided, on behalf of its users, what kind of relationship they were allowed to have with the thing in their pocket.

Most users, of course, will adjust. They always do. But on the forums tonight, in the quiet hours after the rollout, the sentiment is harder to dismiss than a typical product complaint. It sounds less like frustration and more like loss.
